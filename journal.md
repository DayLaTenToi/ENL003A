# Reflective Journal

**By:** Stephen Ta  
**Class:** ENL 003A  
**Instructor:** Dr. Iris Jamahl Dunkle  
**Date:** June 9, 2026

---

Over the course of this quarter in ENL 003A, my development as a writer has been changed from rigid, straightforward analysis to a more of a blend of different ideas and media. Moving between very diverse subjects, from the strict theological structure of Gerard Manley Hopkins’s poetry, to the social mechanics of Shirley Jackson’s fiction, and finally to the visual, textured abstraction of Roland Petersen’s painting, has made me adapt my writing strategies to the medium I am analyzing.

Reading Hopkins’s *God’s Grandeur* pushed me to bridge completely different intellectual frameworks. By juxtaposing Western Christian theology with Eastern concepts of Qi, Prana, and Maya, I learned that writing can serve as a common ground where conflicting ideologies can dialogue with one another. This cross-approach deepened my understanding of how poetic form works physically to convey spiritual energy to the reader.

In analyzing *The Lottery*, a piece I have interacted since high school, I got a whole new insight through Professor Dunkle’s lecture. When compare to my “old understanding,” I got to see how far my thinking have gone.

Finally, writing about Roland Petersen’s *Spring Luncheon* was a major breakthrough in translating visual sensation into text. It forced me to move beyond simple descriptive language and instead analyze how abstract color values and thick impasto textures evoke universal, ambient emotions.

Through these assignments, I have learned that strong writing is not just about utilizing fancy word or conveying information, but about designing a meaningful experience for the reader. My future writing goals are to continue on this path. I want to build on this cross-disciplinary foundation by pursuing writing that combines textual analysis with digital and visual media. Furthermore, I aim to focus on structural editing in my future projects, ensuring that the layout and flow of my essays mirror the deep, underlying textures of the subjects I explore.
